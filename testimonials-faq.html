The UK banking complaints process follows a structured two-stage approach:

  1. Internal Complaint: First, raise your complaint directly with your bank. They have 8 weeks to provide a final response under FCA regulations.
  2. Financial Ombudsman Service: If unsatisfied with the bank's response, you can escalate to the free Financial Ombudsman Service within 6 months.

Our solicitors can assist at both stages, ensuring your complaint is properly documented and presented for maximum chance of success.

Consider seeking legal representation when:

  • The dispute involves significant amounts (typically £1,000+)
  • Your bank has rejected your complaint without proper consideration
  • Complex regulatory issues are involved (PSD2, Consumer Credit Act, etc.)
  • You're facing repossession or account freezing
  • Time limits are approaching (e.g., 6-year limitation periods)
  • The bank's conduct appears to breach FCA principles

Early legal intervention often leads to faster, more favourable outcomes.

UK banking customers have extensive regulatory protections:

  • FCA Principles: Banks must treat customers fairly and act with integrity
  • Payment Services Regulations: Protection for unauthorised transactions
  • Consumer Credit Act: Rights regarding loans and credit agreements
  • Data Protection: GDPR rights over your financial data
  • Vulnerable Customer Rules: Enhanced protections for vulnerable circumstances
  • Right to Complain: Free access to Financial Ombudsman Service

These rights are enforceable through legal action where banks fail to comply.

Business eligibility depends on size and turnover:

For businesses outside Ombudsman scope, we specialise in:

FCA complaints are appropriate for systemic issues affecting multiple customers:

  • Widespread Mis-selling: Products sold inappropriately across customer base
  • Systemic Failures: IT failures, widespread charging errors
  • Regulatory Breaches: Clear violations of FCA rules or principles
  • Consumer Detriment: Practices causing widespread customer harm

Note: FCA complaints don't provide individual compensation - they're for regulatory enforcement. For personal redress, use the Ombudsman Service or court action.

We can coordinate regulatory complaints alongside individual case strategies for maximum impact.

Credit agreements may be unenforceable due to various Consumer Credit Act breaches:

  • Documentation Defects: Missing prescribed terms, incorrect calculations
  • Unfair Relationships: Excessive interest rates, unfair terms under s.140A
  • Inadequate Pre-contract Information: Failure to provide required disclosures
  • Improper Execution: Missing signatures, wrong prescribed forms
  • Breach of Advertising Rules: Misleading representative examples

Unenforceable agreements can result in:

  • Court refusal to enforce debt collection
  • Interest and charges refunds
  • Compensation for unfair relationships

Take immediate action to protect your position:

  1. Report Immediately: Contact your bank within 24 hours if possible
  2. Document Everything: Keep records of all communications and evidence
  3. Request Refund: Banks must refund unauthorised payments under Payment Services Regulations
  4. Challenge Rejections: If the bank claims you authorised payments, seek legal advice

Under Payment Services Regulations 2017, you're entitled to:

  • Immediate refund of unauthorised transactions
  • Maximum liability of £35 for lost/stolen cards (often £0)
  • Full refund where bank systems failed

Banks can close accounts but must follow proper procedures:

  • Notice Period: Usually 2 months' notice required by account terms
  • Legitimate Reasons: Must have valid commercial or regulatory reasons
  • Fair Treatment: Cannot discriminate or act unreasonably
  • Explanation Rights: You can request reasons for closure decisions

Potential challenges include:

  • Discrimination: Closure based on protected characteristics
  • Breach of Contract: Failure to follow account terms
  • Regulatory Breaches: Unfair treatment under FCA principles
  • Data Protection: Incorrect information leading to closure

Business account closures often have more complex commercial implications requiring immediate legal intervention.

Interest rate challenges depend on your loan type and terms:

  • Variable Rate Loans: Banks must follow contractual variation clauses
  • Tracker Mortgages: Must track base rate accurately with agreed margin
  • Standard Variable Rates: More discretion but must act reasonably
  • Fixed Rate Products: Generally cannot be varied during fixed period

Potential grounds for challenge:

  • Contractual Breach: Variation outside agreed terms
  • Unfair Terms: Excessive variation powers under consumer law
  • Regulatory Issues: Failure to treat customers fairly
  • Mis-selling: Incorrect representations about rate changes

Recent FCA guidance requires clearer communication about variable rate changes.

Under GDPR and UK Data Protection Act 2018, you have extensive rights:

  • Right of Access: Obtain copies of all personal data held
  • Right to Rectification: Correct inaccurate information
  • Right to Erasure: Request deletion in certain circumstances
  • Right to Restrict Processing: Limit how data is used
  • Right to Data Portability: Transfer data to another provider

Banks must respond to requests within one month and cannot charge for most requests.

Common data protection issues include:

  • Incorrect credit file information
  • Sharing data with third parties without consent
  • Data breaches affecting customer information
  • Retention of data beyond necessary periods

Data protection breaches can result in ICO complaints and compensation claims.

Time limits vary depending on the type of claim:

  • Contract Claims: 6 years from breach (12 years if under deed)
  • Negligence Claims: 6 years from loss, or 3 years from knowledge
  • Regulatory Complaints: 6 months from bank's final response
  • Consumer Credit Act: 6 years for most claims, 12 years for land charges
  • Data Protection: 6 years for compensation, no limit for rectification

Key limitation principles:

  • Date of Knowledge: When you reasonably knew of the loss
  • Continuing Breaches: Fresh limitation periods for ongoing breaches
  • Concealment: Time may not start until fraud/concealment discovered
  • Disability/Minority: Extended periods for certain circumstances
